Understanding Hierarchical Routing

Welcome to the Hierarchical Routing section of the Routing Algorithm Visualization Hub! Hierarchical routing is a method that divides routers into regions. Each router has complete details about how to route packets within its own region but lacks information about the internal structure of other regions.

As networks grow larger, the size of routing tables in Distance Vector (DV) and Link State (LS) protocols can become unmanageable, leading to inefficiencies in network traffic handling. Hierarchical routing addresses this issue by classifying routers into groups called regions, allowing each router to maintain information only about routers in its region.

For very large networks, a two-level hierarchy may not be sufficient. Therefore, it may be necessary to further categorize regions into clusters, clusters into zones, and zones into groups to optimize routing efficiency.

Steps of Implementation

  • 1. Define Regions
    The network is divided into multiple regions, with routers assigned to specific regions based on their locations or functions.
  • 2. Maintain Regional Information
    Each router maintains a routing table that includes information about routers in its own region but has no knowledge of routers in other regions.
  • 3. Exchange Information
    Routers within the same region periodically share routing information to ensure all routers have up-to-date data.
  • 4. Record Inter-Region Routes
    Each router keeps a record in its table for every other region, allowing it to route packets to other regions when necessary.
  • 5. Hierarchical Management
    As the network grows, routers may be organized into further clusters, zones, and groups to maintain efficiency and manageability.
